# Malaysia Waktu Solat Dataset: Analytical Notes

This document covers what the README does not: where the data originates, why three separate files
ship instead of one flat table, what the distribution numbers tell us about Malaysia's prayer-time
geography, what the Hijri calendar rows actually represent, and what a downstream consumer should
watch out for before relying on any of it. The README is the starting point; this is the background.

---

## How JAKIM publishes prayer times in Malaysia

**Jabatan Kemajuan Islam Malaysia (JAKIM)** is the federal body responsible for Islamic affairs
coordination in Malaysia. One of its outputs is the national prayer-time schedule, the *takwim*,
which it publishes through the **e-Solat** portal at `e-solat.gov.my`. The portal exposes two
relevant API endpoints: one for zone-month prayer-time tables and one for Hijri-Gregorian date
mappings.

JAKIM computes prayer times astronomically rather than observing them in the field. The times in
this dataset are the product of a calculation, not a muezzin watching the horizon. The computation
runs once per zone-month and the result is the published takwim. Individual masjid may start
prayers a few minutes later in practice, but the table is the authoritative published schedule.

The angular thresholds JAKIM uses for fajr (Subuh) and isha (Isyak) follow the **MABIMS
convention**: the standard shared by Brunei, Indonesia, Malaysia, and Singapore. MABIMS sets fajr
at a solar depression angle of 20 degrees (revised downward from 18 in the 2021 update) and isha
at 18 degrees. These thresholds place Subuh and Isyak slightly earlier and later (respectively)
than some other conventions such as ISNA or MWL. The 2021 revision shifted Subuh earlier by
roughly 4-8 minutes depending on latitude and season. Anyone comparing JAKIM times against a
third-party prayer app that defaults to a different convention or has not yet adopted the 2021
MABIMS update will see differences; that is not a data error.

JAKIM partitions Malaysia into **60 zones**. The zone boundaries are geographic rather than
strictly administrative: a single state may contain several zones (Kedah has 7, Sabah has 9), and
a zone may span parts of multiple districts. JAKIM assigns each zone a code following the pattern
`[A-Z]{3}\d{2}` (for example, `SGR01`, `KDH03`, `SBH07`). The first three letters correspond to
an abbreviated state name; the two digits distinguish zones within the state.

Malaysia also has state-level Islamic authorities: JAIS (Selangor), JAINJ (Johor), MAIWP (Wilayah
Persekutuan), JAIS Sabah, JIPN Perak, and others. These bodies sometimes publish their own
prayer-time schedules that differ from JAKIM's by a minute or two, reflecting local authority
discretion. For the national takwim, state authorities defer to JAKIM. This dataset tracks
JAKIM's national schedule only.

The e-Solat portal does not publish API documentation. Both endpoints (`takwimsolat` and
`tarikhtakwim`) are undocumented but have remained structurally stable across the dataset's
collection period. The dataset is generated by hitting each endpoint once per zone-month (for
prayer times) and once per Hijri month (for tarikh) at a single snapshot date.

## Why three files

The decision to ship three files rather than one merged table is deliberate. Each file has a
different update cadence, a different primary audience, and a different natural key.

`prayer_times` is fact-and-time data. Each row represents one JAKIM zone on one calendar day,
carrying eight time columns: Imsak, Subuh, Syuruk, Dhuha, Zohor, Asar, Maghrib, Isyak. The
natural key is `(zone, date)`. 175,200 rows total: 60 zones times roughly 2,920 days each
(2020-2027). This file is the main event for prayer-time apps, scheduling tools, and statistical
analysis.

`zones` is reference data. It has exactly 60 rows describing JAKIM's spatial partitioning: zone
code, state, and location label. This data changes only if JAKIM redraws its zone boundaries,
which has not happened in the collection period. Folding it into every `prayer_times` row would
repeat the same state and location string 2,920 times per zone (once per day across 8 years), or
175,200 repetitions of 60 unique strings. The join is a single line of pandas or one SQL `JOIN`.

`tarikh` is a separate calendar-conversion table with its own independent use cases. It covers
**28 years** (2000-01-01 to 2027-12-28) versus the 8 years of prayer-time data. Apps that need
to display a Hijri date on a calendar widget, legal documents that require Hijri headers, or
historical research that works with pre-2020 dates can use `tarikh` without loading any
prayer-time rows. The primary key is `gregorian`; each row has one Hijri equivalent and a
verified weekday name.

Joining all three into one table would either bloat `prayer_times` with state and location
repetitions on every row, or force consumers who only need calendar conversions to wade through
175,200 prayer-time rows. Three files keeps schemas clean. Joins are two-column merges.

## Distribution observations

A few things worth noting from the numbers.

### Sabah and Sarawak dominate the zone count

Sabah and Sarawak each have **9 JAKIM zones**, the highest of any state. The next-densest states
are Kedah, Pahang, and Perak at 7 zones each. At the other end, Melaka, Perlis, Penang, Kuala
Lumpur (FT), and Labuan (FT) each have exactly one zone.

The Sabah and Sarawak zone counts reflect geographic area and the spread of Muslim communities
across large, varied terrain. Both states cover territories with coastal settlements, interior
communities, and island populations that sit far enough apart to warrant separate zone
computations. Sabah alone spans roughly 4.5 degrees of latitude and 5 degrees of longitude.
JAKIM's per-locality partitioning there is correspondingly fine-grained.

Peninsular states with more compact or more urbanised footprints get fewer zones. Selangor,
despite being the most economically active state in Malaysia, has only 3 JAKIM zones. Penang,
whose main territory is an island plus a thin coastal strip on the mainland, needs only one.
Within a small area the variation in solar angles is below the precision of the published
`HH:MM` time format, so one zone per state is sensible.

In row terms: Sabah and Sarawak each account for 26,280 prayer-time rows (15% each). All five
single-zone states account for 2,920 rows each (1.7% each). The zone-count distribution is not
proportional to population; it tracks geography and settlement pattern.

### East-to-west spread of prayer times

The single most striking feature of this dataset is the spread of prayer times across the country.
On 2026-05-04, Subuh ranged from **04:43** (SBH03, Sabah) to **05:56** (KDH06, Kedah): a spread
of **73 minutes** across 60 zones for a single prayer on a single day. Maghrib that same day
ranged from **18:13** (SBH03) to **19:30** (KDH07, Kedah): a spread of **77 minutes**.

This is the geographic reality of Malaysia. Sabah's easternmost zones sit roughly 13 degrees of
longitude east of Kedah on the northwestern Peninsular coast. At tropical latitudes, 13 degrees of
longitude corresponds to approximately 52 minutes of solar time. The sun rises and sets earlier in
clock time in Sabah than in Penang, and JAKIM's tables capture that honestly.

The reason the spread shows up so clearly in wall-clock time is a political decision made in 1981.
Before that, Peninsular Malaysia used UTC+7:30 and Sabah and Sarawak used UTC+8. Malaysia unified
under a single UTC+8 timezone in 1981 to simplify commerce and government coordination across
the federation. The time zones converged; the sun did not. Sabah's Subuh at 04:43 and Kedah's
Subuh at 05:56 represent the same astronomical event (solar depression angle crossing the MABIMS
fajr threshold) arriving 73 minutes apart in clock time because both clocks are set to UTC+8
despite the two locations being more than a thousand kilometres apart longitudinally.

The spread is not uniform across prayers. Zohor (solar noon) has a smaller clock spread because
it is keyed directly to the sun's meridian transit, which scales linearly with longitude. Subuh
and Isyak have slightly larger spread because the depression-angle calculation is sensitive to
both longitude and the current declination of the sun.

Three zones with the earliest Subuh on 2026-05-04: SBH03 at 04:43, SBH01 at 04:44, SBH05 at
04:46. Three zones with the latest Subuh: PRK05 at 05:55, PNG01 at 05:55, KDH06 at 05:56.
The pattern holds: Sabah leads, the far northwest of Peninsular Malaysia trails.

### Single-zone states

Melaka, Perlis, Penang, Kuala Lumpur (FT), and Labuan (FT) each have exactly one JAKIM zone.

For the federal territories the single-zone treatment fits their footprint. Kuala Lumpur is a
243 km2 city-territory; its entire extent fits within a narrow band of solar angles. Labuan is a
small island off the Sabah coast. One zone per territory is the right granularity.

For Penang and Perlis the single-zone assignment reflects an administrative rather than a strictly
geographic constraint. Both states are small enough that a single prayer-time calculation covers
the entire territory without introducing errors that would show up in `HH:MM` precision. The
within-state variation in sunrise and sunset times is below the rounding threshold of the
published format. A zone with sub-minute prayer-time variation across its extent adds nothing.

### WLY01 covers Kuala Lumpur and Putrajaya jointly

JAKIM does not give Putrajaya its own zone. Zone `WLY01` is described as "Kuala Lumpur,
Putrajaya" and covers both territories. Putrajaya holds its own municipal status as a federal
territory since 2001, but its geographic position is close enough to Kuala Lumpur that the
prayer-time values are identical under JAKIM's computation. A separate zone would be
administratively tidy but scientifically empty.

`WLY02` is Labuan, the second and final Wilayah Persekutuan zone. Despite both being federal
territories, Labuan gets its own zone because it sits off the Sabah coast and its solar position
differs meaningfully from KL and Putrajaya. The two WLY zones are not adjacent geographically;
they share only the federal territory designation.

## Hijri calendar observations

The `tarikh` table ships JAKIM's official Hijri-Gregorian mappings rather than values from an
arithmetic Hijri calendar. The distinction is not cosmetic.

JAKIM's takwim uses **observation-corrected Hijri** (rukyah-adjusted). The start of each Hijri
month follows the visibility of the new crescent moon as declared by JAKIM's moon-sighting
committee. An arithmetic Hijri calendar calculates month starts from a fixed formula based on
mean lunar periods; it does not account for actual observational conditions. The two can differ
by one day at month boundaries when cloud cover, atmospheric conditions, or observer geography
affects the first sighting.

In practice, the majority of month-start dates in JAKIM's takwim align with what an arithmetic
computation would produce. But at Ramadan starts, Syawal starts (Hari Raya Aidilfitri), and
Zulhijjah starts (Aidiladha), JAKIM's declaration sometimes differs from the arithmetic result
by one day. These are the months that attract public attention and where the rukyah determination
is carefully documented. The dataset contains those declaration-based values, not arithmetic
approximations.

Compared to Saudi Arabia's **Umm-al-Qura** calendar, which uses a different astronomical
criterion for month entry (conjunction-based rather than visibility-based), JAKIM's takwim
sometimes disagrees by one day on Ramadan or Zulhijjah starts. This is normal across
Muslim-majority countries. The ASEAN neighbours (Brunei, Indonesia, Singapore) sometimes
agree with Malaysia and sometimes do not, depending on that year's moon-sighting conditions.
The dataset faithfully preserves JAKIM's declarations; consumers who need Umm-al-Qura
alignment will need to reconcile the two sources themselves.

The dataset covers **10,224 rows spanning 2000-01-01 to 2027-12-28**: 28 years of contiguous
JAKIM tarikh data. Years before 2000 are not available through JAKIM's `tarikhtakwim` endpoint.
Data beyond 2027-12-28 is excluded because of known upstream gaps and a misaligned-month bug at
Hijri 1450-04 where the API returns dates offset by a full Hijri year. Those rows are dropped
rather than published with unreliable values.

## Coverage and confidence

**Single source.** Every row in this dataset originates from one upstream registry: JAKIM's
e-Solat API. There is no cross-validation against an independent source. If JAKIM has a
computational drift in one zone, a typo in a zone name, or a systematic error in one month's
calculation, this dataset has the same problem. For critical uses, verify specific values against
the upstream portal directly using the zone code and date as query parameters.

**MABIMS method only.** This dataset ships exactly what JAKIM publishes: prayer times computed
under the MABIMS angular conventions. Other methods (MWL, ISNA, Egyptian, Karachi) produce
different Subuh and Isyak times for the same date and location. The differences can be anywhere
from 5 to 20 minutes depending on method and season. This is not a data error; it is a
deliberate methodological scope. The dataset is JAKIM/MABIMS only.

**Per-masjid offsets exist in practice.** The muezzin at any given masjid may call Subuh a few
minutes after the published JAKIM time. Isyak may begin a few minutes early at some masjid.
These are local jemaah-level conventions, often written into the masjid's own internal schedule.
They are not recorded in any machine-readable published form and they are not a data error.

**Leap-day gap.** JAKIM's `takwimsolat` API consistently returns 28 days for February in leap
years. February 29, 2020 and February 29, 2024 are absent for all 60 zones. Each affected leap
year has 365 prayer-time rows per zone instead of 366. The per-year CSV files are named
accurately, but consumers should expect 21,900 rows per year (not 21,960) for 2020 and 2024.
This gap persists until JAKIM patches their endpoint. It is tracked as an open issue.

**Tarikh range cap at 2027-12-28.** JAKIM's `tarikhtakwim` endpoint has known upstream gaps
starting in late 2027 and the misaligned-month bug at Hijri 1450-04. The `tarikh.csv` is trimmed
to the last fully contiguous and reliable Gregorian date before the glitch. A future refresh
extends the window once JAKIM's published data covers 2028 onward without holes.

**Prayer times end at 2027-12-31.** Future years not yet in JAKIM's published schedule are absent.
Times beyond 2027-12-31 require a new data snapshot, not extrapolation from existing rows.

## Future work

In rough priority order:

1. **Add zone polygon GeoJSON.** JAKIM publishes zone boundaries on the e-Solat site as visual
   maps but not as machine-readable geometries. A polygon file per zone would let downstream
   consumers do point-in-polygon lookups: given a GPS coordinate, return the JAKIM zone code.
   That is the operation every Malaysian prayer-time app actually performs. Digitising the JAKIM
   zone-boundary maps from source imagery is a multi-week effort requiring manual tracing and
   ground-truth verification. It is the highest-value addition for practical use.

2. **Fold in state Islamic authority tables.** JAIS Selangor, JAINJ Johor, MAIWP for the
   Wilayah territories, JAIS Sabah, and JIPN Perak each publish their own takwim, sometimes
   with slight differences from JAKIM's national schedule (often one or two minutes). A second
   compilation comparing JAKIM against these state tables would let apps surface state-specific
   schedules where the local authority is the preferred reference.

3. **Add an arithmetic-versus-observation Hijri delta column.** Compute the pure-arithmetic
   Hijri equivalent for each Gregorian date and ship the signed day-difference alongside JAKIM's
   rukyah-corrected value. Consumers working with arithmetic-calendar systems for legal records
   or date-of-birth contexts could translate cleanly without building their own arithmetic
   calendar implementation.

4. **Cover years before 2000.** JAKIM's API does not expose pre-2000 prayer times. Other
   published Malaysian sources may cover earlier years. Extending backward would also extend
   the tarikh table, which has independent value for genealogical and historical-records work.

5. **Refresh once JAKIM patches the leap-day gap and late-2027 tarikh holes.** Both are tracked
   as open issues for a future refresh. A patched snapshot would restore February 29 rows for
   2020 and 2024 (if the endpoint is fixed) and extend the tarikh range into 2028 and beyond.

## Source attribution

**JAKIM**, Jabatan Kemajuan Islam Malaysia. National prayer-time portal at
`https://www.e-solat.gov.my`. Two API endpoints used:

- `?r=esolatApi/takwimsolat`: zone-month prayer-time tables. Queried once per zone per month
  to build `prayer_times`.
- `?r=esolatApi/tarikhtakwim`: Hijri-Gregorian date mapping by Hijri month. Queried once per
  Hijri month to build `tarikh`.

Both endpoints are undocumented but have been stable across the collection period. This dataset
is generated by hitting them at the snapshot date listed in the README provenance table. The
endpoints are not mirrored here. To verify any individual row, query the e-Solat portal directly
with the corresponding zone code and date.

The factual data (prayer-time values, Hijri dates, zone codes) is the work of JAKIM. What this
dataset adds is the schema design, English snake_case column names, normalised state taxonomy, the
day-of-week correction applied to a small number of late-2027 rows, and the editorial decisions
documented under Provenance in the README. That selection and arrangement is what the CC BY 4.0
licence on this repository covers.
